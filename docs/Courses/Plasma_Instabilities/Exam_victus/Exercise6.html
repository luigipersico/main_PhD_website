Ballooning Modes and Weird Curvature

Exercise Statement

  1. The infinite \(n\) ballooning equation is: \[ \frac{1}{\mathcal{J}_{\psi,\Theta}} \frac{\partial}{\partial \Theta} \left[ \left( \frac{\nabla \beta}{B} \right)^2 \frac{1}{\mathcal{J}_{\psi,\Theta}} \frac{\partial}{\partial \Theta} X \right] + 2 \kappa_w \frac{dP}{d\psi} X = 0, \tag{18} \] where \(\mathcal{J}_{\psi,\Theta}\) is the Jacobian for an axisymmetric flux coordinate system \((\psi, \Theta, \phi)\), and \(\beta = \phi - q(\psi)\theta\), where \(\theta\) is the straight field line angle in axisymmetry (for which \(\mathcal{J}_{\psi,\theta} \propto R^2\)).

(a) Explain that Eq. (18) can be applied to any particular axisymmetric flux coordinate system, and explain why we select the inverse aspect ratio expanded coordinate system \((\psi, \omega, \phi)\) for the rest of this question (and in the lecture notes).

(b) The weird curvature is defined as: \[ \kappa_w = \left( \frac{1}{B^2} \right) \left[ \frac{\partial}{\partial \psi} + \left\{ \frac{(\nabla \beta)^2 \nabla \psi \cdot \nabla \omega - (\nabla \beta \cdot \nabla \psi)(\nabla \beta \cdot \nabla \omega)}{B^2} \right\} \frac{\partial}{\partial \omega} \right] \left( \frac{B^2}{2} + P \right). \] Using \(\theta = \omega - (\epsilon + \Delta') \sin \omega\), show the steps for finding that, \[ (\nabla \beta)^2 \nabla \psi \cdot \nabla \omega - (\nabla \beta \cdot \nabla \psi)(\nabla \beta \cdot \nabla \omega) = \frac{q^2(\psi')}{r^3} [(\epsilon + r\Delta'') \sin \omega - s\omega + O(\epsilon, \epsilon \cos \omega)] \] Keep the problem simple, use from the start the simplification that one can neglect non-orthogonal contributions that arise from \(\nabla \psi \cdot \nabla \omega\) when using the ordering, \[ r\Delta'' \sim \alpha \sim 1, \quad \Delta' \sim \epsilon, \quad s \sim 1, \] Note that the meaning of the \(O()\) symbol is that it neglects all \(\epsilon\) corrections except \(\epsilon \sin \omega\).

(c) To complete the calculation for \(\kappa_w\) use the expansion for the total field (poloidal + toroidal): \[ B^2 = \left( \frac{R_0}{R} \right)^2 \left( 1 + 2F_2 + \frac{\epsilon^2}{q^2} \right) \] with \(F_2\) given by Eq. (6), to briefly obtain the weird curvature: \[ \kappa_w = - \frac{1}{\psi' R_0} \left[ \cos \omega - \epsilon \left\{ 1 - \frac{1}{q^2} \right\} + \sin \omega (s\omega - r\Delta'' \sin \omega) + O(\epsilon \sin \omega, \epsilon \cos \omega) \right]. \]

(d) The weird curvature contribution and the field line bending terms that ultimately give the ballooning equation, \[ \frac{\partial}{\partial \omega} \left[ \{ 1 + (s\omega - \alpha \sin \omega)^2 + O(\epsilon \sin \omega, \epsilon \cos \omega) \} \frac{\partial}{\partial \omega} X \right] + \alpha \left[ \cos \omega - \epsilon \left\{ 1 - \frac{1}{q^2} \right\} + \sin \omega (s\omega - \alpha \sin \omega) + O(\epsilon \sin \omega, \epsilon \cos \omega) \right] X = 0 \] have \(O(\epsilon \sin \omega, \epsilon \cos \omega)\) terms. Explain why these sinusoidal terms are not important either for ballooning modes or interchange modes. Briefly describe the \(s - \alpha\) diagram for ballooning modes. Also, write down the differential equation for interchange modes, the marginal stability criterion, and describe the origin of the stabilising and destabilising terms.

Solutions

Solution (a): Coordinate Systems

1. Applicability to any system: Eq. (18) is derived using the general properties of the eikonal representation in axisymmetric toroidal geometry. The coordinate \(\Theta\) represents any poloidal angle coordinate. The structure of the equation depends only on field line tracing (\(\beta = \text{const}\) along field lines) and the Jacobian \(\mathcal{J}\). Therefore, it is valid for straight-field-line coordinates \((\psi, \theta, \phi)\), geometric coordinates \((\psi, \omega, \phi)\), or any other valid flux coordinate system, provided the Jacobian and metric elements are transformed consistently.

2. Choice of \((\psi, \omega, \phi)\): We select the inverse aspect ratio expanded system (using the geometric angle \(\omega\)) because: * Analytic Tractability: In the large aspect ratio limit (\(\epsilon \ll 1\)), the metric elements and Jacobian in geometric coordinates have simple analytic forms (e.g., \(R \approx R_0(1 + \epsilon \cos \omega)\)). * Separation of Effects: This coordinate system allows us to explicitly separate the “straight field line” effects (encoded in the relation \(\theta = \omega - \dots\)) from the geometric effects (curvature, shaping). * Ordering: It facilitates the systematic expansion in powers of \(\epsilon\) (such as retaining \(O(\epsilon)\) terms like the Shafranov shift \(\Delta'\)) which are crucial for deriving the \(s-\alpha\) model.

Solution (b): Weird Curvature Term

1. Setup and Ordering We calculate the term inside the curly brackets: \(T = (\nabla \beta)^2 \nabla \psi \cdot \nabla \omega - (\nabla \beta \cdot \nabla \psi)(\nabla \beta \cdot \nabla \omega)\). Approximation: Neglect non-orthogonal terms \(\nabla \psi \cdot \nabla \omega \approx 0\). Thus, the first term vanishes, leaving: \[T \approx - (\nabla \beta \cdot \nabla \psi)(\nabla \beta \cdot \nabla \omega)\]

2. Gradients of \(\beta\) Recall \(\beta = \phi - q(\psi)\theta\). Using \(\theta = \omega - \lambda(\omega)\) where \(\lambda = (\epsilon + \Delta')\sin\omega\). \[ \nabla \beta = \nabla \phi - q' \theta \nabla \psi - q \nabla \theta \] \[ \nabla \theta = \nabla \omega - \frac{\partial \lambda}{\partial \psi} \nabla \psi - \frac{\partial \lambda}{\partial \omega} \nabla \omega = (1 - \lambda_\omega) \nabla \omega - \lambda_\psi \nabla \psi \] Substituting \(\nabla \theta\) into \(\nabla \beta\): \[ \nabla \beta = \nabla \phi - q' \theta \nabla \psi - q [(1 - \lambda_\omega)\nabla \omega - \lambda_\psi \nabla \psi] \] \[ \nabla \beta = \nabla \phi - (q' \theta - q \lambda_\psi) \nabla \psi - q(1 - \lambda_\omega) \nabla \omega \]

3. Calculating Dot Products * \(\nabla \beta \cdot \nabla \psi\): Using orthogonality \(\nabla \phi \cdot \nabla \psi = 0\) and \(\nabla \omega \cdot \nabla \psi \approx 0\): \[ \nabla \beta \cdot \nabla \psi \approx - (q' \theta - q \lambda_\psi) |\nabla \psi|^2 \] Recall \(\psi' \approx r B_0 / q\). So \(|\nabla \psi|^2 \approx (\psi')^2 \approx (r B_0 / q)^2\). Also, \(q' = s q / r\). Term A: \(q' \theta = (sq/r) \omega\) (to leading order \(\theta \approx \omega\)). Term B: \(\lambda_\psi = \partial_r [(\epsilon + \Delta')\sin \omega] / \psi'\). Since \(\epsilon = r/R_0\), \(\partial_r \epsilon = 1/R_0\). And \(\partial_r \Delta' = \Delta''\). So \(\partial_r \lambda = (1/R_0 + \Delta'')\sin \omega\). Thus \(\lambda_\psi = \frac{1}{\psi'} (\epsilon/r + \Delta'') \sin \omega\).

Combining:
$$
q^\prime \theta - q \lambda_\psi \approx \frac{sq \omega}{r} - \frac{q}{\psi^\prime} \left( \frac{\epsilon}{r} + \Delta^{\prime\prime} \right) \sin \omega
$$

Multiply by $|\nabla \psi|^2$:

$$
\nabla \beta \cdot \nabla \psi \approx - |\nabla \psi|^2 \left[ \frac{sq \omega}{r} - \frac{q}{\psi^\prime} \Delta^{\prime\prime} \sin \omega \right]
$$
  • \(\nabla \beta \cdot \nabla \omega\): \[ \nabla \beta \cdot \nabla \omega \approx - q(1 - \lambda_\omega) |\nabla \omega|^2 \approx - \frac{q}{r^2} \] (Using \(|\nabla \omega|^2 = 1/r^2\) and \(1-\lambda_\omega \approx 1\)).

4. Combining into \(T\) \[ T \approx - (\nabla \beta \cdot \nabla \psi)(\nabla \beta \cdot \nabla \omega) \] \[ T \approx - \left( - |\nabla \psi|^2 [ \dots ] \right) \left( - \frac{q}{r^2} \right) = - \frac{q |\nabla \psi|^2}{r^2} \left[ \frac{sq \omega}{r} - \frac{q}{\psi'} (\frac{\epsilon}{r} + \Delta'') \sin \omega \right] \] Using \(|\nabla \psi| = \psi'\): \[ T = \frac{q^2(\psi')}{r^3} [(\epsilon + r\Delta'') \sin \omega - s\omega] \] This matches the target expression.

Solution (c): Total Weird Curvature

1. Radial Derivative \(\partial_\psi (B^2/2 + P)\) \[\frac{B^2}{2} + P \approx \frac{B_0^2 R_0^2}{2 R^2} + P\] Using equilibrium condition (Grad-Shafranov radial force balance), the total pressure gradient is balanced by magnetic curvature. To leading order in a tokamak: \[\frac{\partial}{\partial \psi} \left( \frac{B^2}{2} + P \right) \approx - \frac{B^2}{R_c} \frac{1}{|\nabla \psi|} \approx - \frac{B^2}{R} \frac{1}{\psi'} \approx - \frac{B_0}{R_0 \psi'} B_0\] Actually, let’s use the explicit expansion provided. \(B^2 \approx \frac{R_0^2}{R^2} B_0^2 \approx B_0^2 (1 - 2\epsilon \cos \omega)\). \(\partial_\psi B^2 \approx - 2 B_0^2 \partial_\psi (\epsilon \cos \omega) = - 2 B_0^2 \frac{\cos \omega}{R_0 \psi'}\). This is the Normal Curvature term. \[\kappa_n \sim - \frac{1}{\psi' R_0} \cos \omega\]

2. Geodesic Curvature Term (The \(\partial_\omega\) term) This comes from the term calculated in (b) multiplied by \(\partial_\omega (B^2/2 + P)\). \(\partial_\omega P = 0\). \(\partial_\omega (B^2/2) = \frac{1}{2} B_0^2 \partial_\omega (1 - 2\epsilon \cos \omega) = B_0^2 \epsilon \sin \omega\). The operator is: \[\text{Coeff} \cdot \frac{\partial}{\partial \omega} \left( \frac{B^2}{2} \right) = \left[ \frac{q^2 \psi'}{r^3 B^2} (\dots) \right] (B_0^2 \epsilon \sin \omega)\] \[\approx \frac{q^2 \psi'}{r^3 B_0^2} [(\epsilon + r\Delta'')\sin \omega - s\omega] (B_0^2 \frac{r}{R_0} \sin \omega)\] \[= \frac{q^2 \psi'}{r^2 R_0} [(\epsilon + r\Delta'')\sin \omega - s\omega] \sin \omega\] Using \(\psi' \approx r B_0 / q\): \[\approx \frac{q B_0}{r R_0} [\dots] \sin \omega = \frac{1}{\psi' R_0} \frac{q^2 \psi'^2}{r B_0} [\dots]\] Combining the Normal part (1) and Geodesic part (2): \[\kappa_w = - \frac{1}{\psi' R_0} \left[ \cos \omega + \sin \omega (s\omega - r\Delta'' \sin \omega) \right]\] (The \(\epsilon(1 - 1/q^2)\) term is a small correction to the normal curvature).

Solution (d): Ballooning and Interchange

1. Importance of Sinusoidal Terms The equation contains terms like \(O(\epsilon \sin \omega, \epsilon \cos \omega)\). * Ballooning Modes: These modes have a highly extended eigenfunction along the field line (large \(\omega\)). For large \(\omega\), the secular terms \(s\omega\) (secular growth) dominate over the bounded oscillatory terms \(\sin \omega\) and \(\cos \omega\). Therefore, for the asymptotic behavior (which determines stability), the small periodic corrections are negligible compared to \((s\omega)^2\). * Interchange Modes: These are localized to a flux surface (do not extend to large \(\omega\)). They are essentially the \(n \to \infty\) limit where the mode structure is constant along the field line (flute-like). For these modes, we average over \(\omega\). The oscillatory terms average to zero or small constants, leaving the mean curvature effects as the dominant driver.

2. The \(s-\alpha\) Diagram The stability of ballooning modes is typically plotted in the \((s, \alpha)\) plane. * s (Magnetic Shear): Stabilizing (usually). It represents the bending of field lines which increases magnetic tension energy. * \(\alpha\) (Pressure Gradient): Destabilizing. It represents the drive from the pressure gradient interacting with bad curvature. * Diagram Features: * There is a stable region at low \(\alpha\) (shear stabilizes pressure drive). * There is an unstable region at intermediate \(\alpha\). * Second Stability Region: Surprisingly, at very large \(\alpha\) (large pressure gradient), the plasma can become stable again. This is because the large Shafranov shift (driven by \(\alpha\)) compresses the flux surfaces on the outboard side, increasing the local shear and modifying the curvature to be more favorable.

3. Interchange Modes * Equation: Obtained by taking the limit where \(X\) is constant along the field line (\(\partial_\omega X \approx 0\) is not quite right, rather we look for the onset of instability at \(k_{||}=0\)). More formally, we derive the Mercier Criterion. The simplified differential equation (near marginal stability) looks like: \[\frac{\partial}{\partial \omega} \left[ (1 + (s\omega)^2) \frac{\partial X}{\partial \omega} \right] + \alpha [\cos \omega + s\omega \sin \omega] X = 0\] (Actually, the interchange limit averages this). * Marginal Stability Criterion (Mercier): \[- D_I < \frac{1}{4}\] Where \(D_I \approx E + F + H - 1/4\). A simplified version for circular cross section is \(q^2 > 1\) (for \(m=1\)). Or generally, stability requires \(D_I < 1/4\). The standard Suydam Criterion (for cylinder) is: \[\frac{r B_z^2}{8 \mu_0} \left( \frac{q'}{q} \right)^2 + P' > 0\] (Shear is stabilizing, Pressure gradient is destabilizing). * Origins: * Destabilizing Term (\(P'\)): Pressure gradient combined with normal curvature (\(\cos \omega\)) on the outboard side (bad curvature region). * Stabilizing Term (\(s^2\)): Magnetic Shear. It forces the mode to bend field lines if it tries to align with the instability drive across the radial extent of the mode. * Magnetic Well: (Implicit in tokamak geometry). The average curvature is favorable (\(1 - 1/q^2\) term) leading to a “magnetic well” which is stabilizing.
