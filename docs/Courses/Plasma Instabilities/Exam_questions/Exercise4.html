External Kink Mode Stability

Exercise Statement

  1. The external kink mode has the total potential energy \(\delta W = \delta W_P + \delta W_V\), where \[ \delta W_P = \frac{2\pi^2 B_0^2}{R_0} \left\{ \int_0^a dr \ r \left[ \left( r \frac{d\xi_0^r}{dr} \right)^2 + (m^2 - 1) (\xi_0^r)^2 \right] \left( \frac{n}{m} - \frac{1}{q} \right)^2 + a^2 (\xi_0^r(a))^2 \left[ \frac{2}{q_a} \left( \frac{n}{m} - \frac{1}{q_a} \right) + \left( \frac{n}{m} - \frac{1}{q_a} \right)^2 \right] \right\} \tag{10} \] with \(q_a = q(a)\), and \[ \delta W_V \approx 2\pi^2 R_0 \left[ r \delta \psi \frac{d\delta \psi}{dr} \right]_a^b \tag{11} \]

(a) The perturbed flux in the vacuum region has the form, \[ \delta \psi = \alpha r^m + \beta r^{-m} \tag{12} \] where \(\delta B^r = i m \delta \psi / r\). Apply the boundary conditions at the plasma boundary \(r=a\) and the radius of the perfectly conducting wall \(r=b\) to obtain, \[ \delta \psi = \frac{B_0}{R_0} \left( \frac{n}{m} - \frac{1}{q_a} \right) \frac{(\frac{r}{b})^m - (\frac{b}{r})^m}{(\frac{a}{b})^m - (\frac{b}{a})^m} a \xi_a \tag{13} \]

(b) Show that, \[ \delta W = \frac{2\pi^2 B_0^2}{R_0} \left\{ \int_0^a dr \ r \left[ \left( r \frac{d\xi_0^r}{dr} \right)^2 + (m^2 - 1)^2 (\xi_0^r)^2 \right] \left( \frac{n}{m} - \frac{1}{q} \right)^2 + a^2 \xi_0^r(a)^2 \left[ \frac{2}{q_a} \left( \frac{n}{m} - \frac{1}{q_a} \right) + (1 + m\lambda) \left( \frac{n}{m} - \frac{1}{q_a} \right)^2 \right] \right\} \tag{14} \] where \[ \lambda = \frac{1 + (a/b)^{2m}}{1 - (a/b)^{2m}} \]

Which term in \(\delta W\) can be destabilising? What is the condition for \(q_a\) in order for that term to be destabilising? What are the effects of and physical meanings of the limits \(b/a \to \infty\) and \(b/a \to 1\)? How does one establish (from \(\delta W\) in Eq. (14)) whether the plasma is unstable to external kink modes? What is the main stabilising effect that can be determined with this procedure, and how can the plasma be made more stable even if \(q_a\) can’t be adjusted?

Solutions

Solution (a): Vacuum Flux Solution

1. Apply Boundary Condition at the Wall (\(r=b\)) The conducting wall at \(r=b\) requires the normal component of the perturbed magnetic field to vanish: \(\delta B^r(b) = 0\). Given \(\delta B^r = im \delta \psi / r\), this implies \(\delta \psi(b) = 0\). Substituting this into the general form (Eq. 12): \[\alpha b^m + \beta b^{-m} = 0 \implies \beta = - \alpha b^{2m}\] Substituting \(\beta\) back into Eq. (12): \[\delta \psi(r) = \alpha (r^m - b^{2m} r^{-m}) = \alpha r^m \left[ 1 - \left( \frac{b}{r} \right)^{2m} \right]\]

2. Apply Boundary Condition at the Plasma Surface (\(r=a\)) At the plasma interface, the radial magnetic field perturbation must be continuous. From the plasma side (internal solution), using the result from the previous exercise: \[\delta B^r(a) = \frac{i B_0}{R_0 q_a} [n q_a - m] \xi_a = \frac{i B_0}{R_0} \left( n - \frac{m}{q_a} \right) \xi_a\] From the vacuum side: \[\delta B^r(a) = \frac{i m \delta \psi(a)}{a}\] Equating the two expressions: \[\frac{i m \delta \psi(a)}{a} = \frac{i B_0}{R_0} m \left( \frac{n}{m} - \frac{1}{q_a} \right) \xi_a\] Solving for \(\delta \psi(a)\): \[\delta \psi(a) = \frac{a B_0}{R_0} \left( \frac{n}{m} - \frac{1}{q_a} \right) \xi_a\]

3. Construct the Final Solution We express the radial dependence using the form derived in step 1, normalized to the value at \(a\): \[\delta \psi(r) = \delta \psi(a) \frac{r^m - b^{2m}r^{-m}}{a^m - b^{2m}a^{-m}}\] To match Eq. (13), we multiply the numerator and denominator by \(b^{-m}\): \[\frac{b^{-m}(r^m - b^{2m}r^{-m})}{b^{-m}(a^m - b^{2m}a^{-m})} = \frac{(r/b)^m - (b/r)^m}{(a/b)^m - (b/a)^m}\] Substituting \(\delta \psi(a)\): \[\delta \psi(r) = \frac{B_0}{R_0} \left( \frac{n}{m} - \frac{1}{q_a} \right) a \xi_a \left[ \frac{(\frac{r}{b})^m - (\frac{b}{r})^m}{(\frac{a}{b})^m - (\frac{b}{a})^m} \right]\]

Solution (b): Total Potential Energy

1. Calculate Vacuum Energy \(\delta W_V\) We evaluate the integral in Eq. (11): \[\delta W_V \approx 2\pi^2 R_0 \left[ r \delta \psi \frac{d\delta \psi}{dr} \right]_a^b\] Since \(\delta \psi(b) = 0\), the upper limit vanishes. \[\delta W_V = - 2\pi^2 R_0 \left( a \delta \psi(a) \delta \psi'(a) \right)\]

First, find the logarithmic derivative \(\frac{\delta \psi'(a)}{\delta \psi(a)}\). Let \(f(r) = (r/b)^m - (b/r)^m\). Then \(\delta \psi(r) \propto f(r)\). \[f'(r) = \frac{m}{b} (r/b)^{m-1} + \frac{m}{r} (b/r)^m = \frac{m}{r} \left[ (r/b)^m + (b/r)^m \right]\] \[\frac{\delta \psi'(a)}{\delta \psi(a)} = \frac{f'(a)}{f(a)} = \frac{m}{a} \frac{(a/b)^m + (b/a)^m}{(a/b)^m - (b/a)^m}\] Multiply numerator and denominator by \((a/b)^m\): \[\frac{(a/b)^{2m} + 1}{(a/b)^{2m} - 1} = - \frac{1 + (a/b)^{2m}}{1 - (a/b)^{2m}} = - \lambda\] Thus, \(\delta \psi'(a) = - \frac{m}{a} \lambda \delta \psi(a)\).

Substitute back into the energy equation: \[\delta W_V = - 2\pi^2 R_0 a \delta \psi(a) \left( - \frac{m}{a} \lambda \delta \psi(a) \right) = 2\pi^2 R_0 m \lambda (\delta \psi(a))^2\]

Now substitute the expression for \(\delta \psi(a)\) derived in Part (a): \[\delta W_V = 2\pi^2 R_0 m \lambda \left[ \frac{a^2 B_0^2}{R_0^2} \left( \frac{n}{m} - \frac{1}{q_a} \right)^2 \xi_a^2 \right]\] \[\delta W_V = \frac{2\pi^2 B_0^2}{R_0} a^2 \xi_a^2 \left[ m \lambda \left( \frac{n}{m} - \frac{1}{q_a} \right)^2 \right]\]

2. Combine with Plasma Energy \(\delta W_P\) Add the \(\delta W_V\) term to the surface terms (coefficients of \(a^2 \xi_a^2\)) in Eq. (10). Existing surface term: \[\left[ \frac{2}{q_a} \left( \frac{n}{m} - \frac{1}{q_a} \right) + \left( \frac{n}{m} - \frac{1}{q_a} \right)^2 \right]\] Adding vacuum term \(m \lambda (\dots)^2\): \[\frac{2}{q_a} \left( \frac{n}{m} - \frac{1}{q_a} \right) + (1 + m \lambda) \left( \frac{n}{m} - \frac{1}{q_a} \right)^2\] This matches Eq. (14) exactly.

Analysis and Interpretation

1. Destabilizing Term The only term in Eq. (14) that can be negative (destabilizing) is the linear term in the boundary bracket: \[\frac{2}{q_a} \left( \frac{n}{m} - \frac{1}{q_a} \right)\] The integral term is always positive (stabilizing), and the quadratic term \((1+m\lambda)(\dots)^2\) is always positive.

2. Condition for Destabilization For the term to be negative, we require: \[\frac{n}{m} - \frac{1}{q_a} < 0 \implies \frac{n}{m} < \frac{1}{q_a} \implies n q_a < m\] Instability generally requires the edge safety factor \(q_a\) to be less than the rational surface value \(m/n\).

3. Effect of Wall Position (\(b/a\)) * \(b/a \to \infty\) (Wall at infinity): \[\lambda \to \frac{1+0}{1-0} = 1\] The vacuum energy is minimized, providing the least amount of stabilization. * \(b/a \to 1\) (Wall touching plasma): \[\lambda \to \frac{2}{0} \to \infty\] The stabilizing term \((1+m\lambda)(\dots)^2\) becomes infinite. This forces \(\delta W\) to be positive (stable) unless \(\xi_a = 0\). This is the phenomenon of wall stabilization.

4. Establishing Instability To prove instability, one must find a trial displacement function \(\xi(r)\) that makes the total potential energy \(\delta W < 0\). If \(\delta W\) can be made negative, the system is unstable.

5. Stabilizing Effects * Main Effect: The presence of the conducting wall (represented by \(\lambda\)) is a major stabilizing factor for the external kink mode. * Stabilization Strategy: If \(q_a\) cannot be adjusted (e.g., to satisfy \(q_a > m/n\)), the plasma can be made more stable by bringing the conducting wall closer to the plasma surface (decreasing \(b/a\)). This increases \(\lambda\) and consequently increases the vacuum magnetic energy penalty for kinking.
