Derivation of Wave Energy Density

Derivation of Brillouin’s Formula

Goal: Derive the expression for the time-averaged energy density of a monochromatic wave in a dispersive medium: \[ \langle W \rangle = \frac{1}{4} \left[ \vec{E}_0^* \cdot \frac{\partial(\omega \hat{\epsilon})}{\partial \omega} \cdot \vec{E}_0 + \vec{H}_0^* \cdot \frac{\partial(\omega \hat{\mu})}{\partial \omega} \cdot \vec{H}_0 \right] \]

1. Poynting’s Theorem

We start with the conservation of energy for electromagnetic fields (Poynting’s Theorem). The rate of work done by the fields on the current density \(\vec{J}\) is: \[ \vec{J} \cdot \vec{E} = - \nabla \cdot (\vec{E} \times \vec{H}) - \vec{E} \cdot \frac{\partial \vec{D}}{\partial t} - \vec{H} \cdot \frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t} \] The term \(\vec{J} \cdot \vec{E}\) represents the power absorbed by the medium. We are interested in the energy stored in a lossless wave packet, so we look at the time rate of change of energy density \(\frac{\partial W}{\partial t}\).

For a dispersive medium, we cannot simply write \(W = \frac{1}{2}(\vec{E}\cdot\vec{D} + \vec{B}\cdot\vec{H})\). Instead, we must analyze a wave packet that grows slowly in time to accumulate energy.

2. Slowly Varying Envelope Approximation

Consider a quasi-monochromatic wave with a slowly varying amplitude \(\vec{E}_0(t)\) (slow compared to the frequency \(\omega\)). The electric field is: \[ \vec{E}(t) = \text{Re} \left[ \vec{E}_0(t) e^{-i\omega t} \right] \] The displacement field \(\vec{D}\) is related to \(\vec{E}\) by the permittivity \(\hat{\epsilon}(\omega)\). Since the amplitude is time-dependent, we expand \(\hat{\epsilon}\) around the carrier frequency \(\omega\) using a Taylor series for the time derivative operator \(\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \to -i\omega + \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\) acting on the envelope.

However, a cleaner approach is to work in the frequency domain. The displacement field is: \[ \vec{D}(t) = \text{Re} \left[ \hat{\epsilon}(\omega) \vec{E}_0(t) e^{-i\omega t} + i \frac{\partial \hat{\epsilon}}{\partial \omega} \frac{d\vec{E}_0}{dt} e^{-i\omega t} + \dots \right] \] (The second term arises because the frequency spectrum of a varying pulse is not a delta function; the derivative \(\partial \epsilon / \partial \omega\) accounts for the “spread” of frequencies).

3. Calculating the Power Density

We calculate the electric contribution to the power density term \(\vec{E} \cdot \frac{\partial \vec{D}}{\partial t}\). Using the complex notation \(A(t) = \frac{1}{2}(A_0 e^{-i\omega t} + A_0^* e^{i\omega t})\): \[ \langle \vec{E} \cdot \frac{\partial \vec{D}}{\partial t} \rangle = \frac{1}{4} \left( \vec{E}_0^* \cdot \frac{\partial \vec{D}_0}{\partial t} + \vec{E}_0 \cdot \frac{\partial \vec{D}_0^*}{\partial t} \right) \] where \(\vec{D}_0 \approx \epsilon(\omega) \vec{E}_0\). However, explicitly keeping the slow time variation allows us to identify the stored energy. The dominant term for energy accumulation involves the time derivative of the amplitude.

Let’s evaluate \(\frac{\partial \vec{D}}{\partial t}\) keeping the first order correction: \[ \frac{\partial \vec{D}}{\partial t} \approx \text{Re} \left[ -i\omega \epsilon \vec{E}_0 e^{-i\omega t} + \frac{\partial (\omega \epsilon)}{\partial \omega} \frac{d\vec{E}_0}{dt} e^{-i\omega t} \right] \] Now, take the dot product with \(\vec{E}(t)\) and time average over one period \(T = 2\pi/\omega\). Fast oscillating terms like \(e^{-2i\omega t}\) average to zero. We are left with cross terms involving \(\vec{E}_0^* \cdot \frac{d\vec{E}_0}{dt}\).

The time-averaged rate of change of energy is: \[ \left\langle \vec{E} \cdot \frac{\partial \vec{D}}{\partial t} \right\rangle = \frac{1}{4} \vec{E}_0^* \cdot \frac{\partial (\omega \epsilon)}{\partial \omega} \cdot \frac{d\vec{E}_0}{dt} + \text{c.c.} \] Assuming the medium is lossless (Hermitian \(\epsilon\)), this can be rewritten as a total time derivative: \[ \left\langle \vec{E} \cdot \frac{\partial \vec{D}}{\partial t} \right\rangle = \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \left( \frac{1}{4} \vec{E}_0^* \cdot \frac{\partial (\omega \epsilon)}{\partial \omega} \cdot \vec{E}_0 \right) \]

4. Final Energy Density Expression

The quantity inside the time derivative is the stored electric energy density. \[ \langle W_E \rangle = \frac{1}{4} \vec{E}_0^* \cdot \frac{\partial (\omega \epsilon)}{\partial \omega} \cdot \vec{E}_0 \] An identical derivation applies to the magnetic term \(\vec{H} \cdot \frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t}\), yielding: \[ \langle W_M \rangle = \frac{1}{4} \vec{H}_0^* \cdot \frac{\partial (\omega \mu)}{\partial \omega} \cdot \vec{H}_0 \] Summing these gives the total wave energy density (Eq 12.1).
