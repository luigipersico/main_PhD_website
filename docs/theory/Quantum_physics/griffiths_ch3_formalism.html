Hilbert-Space Formalism for Quantum Measurements (Griffiths Ch. 3 — Notes)

Author

Luigi Persico

Published

March 5, 2026

1 Scope and goal

These notes summarize Chapter 3 (“Formalism”) of Introduction to Quantum Mechanics (Griffiths & Schroeter, 3rd ed., 2018).

Big picture: quantum mechanics is (i) a state space (Hilbert space), (ii) observables as Hermitian operators, and (iii) a measurement postulate giving outcomes and probabilities. Once this is in place, tools like uncertainty relations, changes of basis, and Dirac notation become natural.

2 Notation conventions

  • State of the system: a vector \(\psi\) in a complex Hilbert space.
  • In the position basis, the state has components \(\psi(x)\).
  • Inner product: \(\langle f, g \rangle = \int f^*(x)\, g(x)\, dx\) (for wavefunctions).
  • Expectation value: \(\langle A \rangle = \langle \psi, \hat A \psi \rangle\).
  • Standard deviation (uncertainty): \(\Delta A = \sqrt{\langle (\hat A-\langle A\rangle)^2\rangle}\).
  • Commutator: \([\hat A,\hat B] = \hat A\hat B - \hat B\hat A\).

3 1. Hilbert space

3.1 1.1 Vector space of states (why we restrict it)

Wavefunctions must be normalizable so that probabilities make sense: \[ \int |\psi(x)|^2\,dx = 1. \] The natural setting is the space of square-integrable functions (physicists’ Hilbert space).

3.2 1.2 Inner product and norm

Define inner product (for functions): \[ \langle f,g\rangle = \int f^*(x)\,g(x)\,dx, \] and norm: \[ \|f\| = \sqrt{\langle f,f\rangle}. \] Key properties: linearity in the second slot, conjugate symmetry, and positivity.

3.3 1.3 Orthonormal sets and completeness

  • Normalized: \(\langle f,f\rangle=1\)
  • Orthogonal: \(\langle f,g\rangle=0\)
  • Orthonormal family \(\{f_n\}\): \(\langle f_m,f_n\rangle=\delta_{mn}\)

A set \(\{f_n\}\) is complete if any vector \(f\) can be expanded: \[ f = \sum_n c_n f_n. \] If the basis is orthonormal, coefficients follow from “Fourier’s trick”: \[ c_n = \langle f_n, f\rangle. \]

3.4 1.4 Schwarz inequality (workhorse inequality)

For any \(f,g\), \[ |\langle f,g\rangle|^2 \le \langle f,f\rangle\,\langle g,g\rangle. \] This single inequality is the engine behind uncertainty relations.

4 2. Observables and Hermitian operators

4.1 2.1 Observables as operators

An observable \(Q\) is represented by a linear operator \(\hat Q\) acting on states: \[ \psi \mapsto \hat Q\psi. \]

4.2 2.2 Expectation values force Hermiticity

The expectation value of a measurable quantity must be real: \[ \langle Q\rangle = \langle \psi, \hat Q\psi\rangle \in \mathbb{R}. \] This holds for all \(\psi\) only if \(\hat Q\) is Hermitian (self-adjoint in physicists’ usage).

4.3 2.3 Definition of Hermitian operator and adjoint

The adjoint \(\hat Q^\dagger\) is defined by: \[ \langle f,\hat Q g\rangle = \langle \hat Q^\dagger f, g\rangle \quad \forall f,g. \] Hermitian means: \[ \hat Q^\dagger = \hat Q. \]

4.4 2.4 Practical note: domains and boundary terms

For differential operators, Hermiticity often depends on:

  • boundary conditions (finite intervals),
  • decay at infinity (infinite line),
  • the domain of allowed functions.

5 3. Determinate states and the eigenvalue equation

5.1 3.1 “Determinate state” = zero spread

A state is determinate for observable \(Q\) if every measurement returns the same value \(q\). Then: \[ \Delta Q = 0. \] Using \(\Delta Q^2 = \langle (\hat Q-\langle Q\rangle)^2\rangle\), this implies: \[ (\hat Q - q)\psi = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \hat Q\psi = q\psi. \]

5.2 3.2 Eigenvalues, spectrum, degeneracy

  • \(q\) is an eigenvalue, \(\psi\) an eigenstate/eigenfunction.
  • The set of all eigenvalues is the spectrum.
  • If multiple linearly independent eigenstates share the same eigenvalue, it’s degenerate.

Physical meaning: If the system is in an eigenstate of \(\hat Q\), then measuring \(Q\) is certain to yield the associated eigenvalue.

6 4. Eigenfunctions of a Hermitian operator

6.1 4.1 Discrete spectra: the “matrix-like” case

For normalizable eigenfunctions:

Theorem 1 (Reality): eigenvalues of a Hermitian operator are real.
Theorem 2 (Orthogonality): eigenfunctions with distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal.

Degenerate subspaces can be orthonormalized (e.g., Gram–Schmidt), so we usually assume an orthonormal eigenbasis.

Completeness is crucial; in infinite dimensions it’s not always trivial to prove, so it’s taken as an axiom/restriction for “physical” observables: \[ \psi = \sum_n c_n\,\psi_n, \qquad c_n=\langle \psi_n,\psi\rangle. \]

6.2 4.2 Continuous spectra: “Dirac orthonormality”

For operators with continuous spectra, eigenfunctions are typically not square-integrable (e.g., plane waves, delta functions). Still, we use a consistent extension:

  • Kronecker delta \(\delta_{mn}\) becomes Dirac delta \(\delta(z-z')\).
  • Sums become integrals.

Example: momentum operator \(\hat p=-i\hbar\,\partial_x\) has eigenfunctions \(e^{ipx/\hbar}\) (not normalizable), but we enforce: \[ \langle p|p'\rangle = \delta(p-p'). \]

Example: position operator \(\hat x\) has eigenfunctions \(\delta(x-y)\) with: \[ \langle x|x'\rangle = \delta(x-x'). \]

Completeness (continuous resolution of identity): \[ \int |z\rangle\langle z|\,dz = \hat I. \]

7 5. Generalized statistical interpretation (measurement postulate)

This is the “measurement rule” in its most general form.

7.1 5.1 Possible outcomes

If you measure observable \(Q\) on a system in state \(|\psi\rangle\), the outcome must be one of the eigenvalues of \(\hat Q\).

7.2 5.2 Probabilities: discrete spectrum

If \(\hat Q\) has orthonormal eigenstates \(|q_n\rangle\), \[ |\psi\rangle = \sum_n c_n |q_n\rangle, \qquad c_n=\langle q_n|\psi\rangle, \] then: \[ P(q_n)=|c_n|^2. \]

7.3 5.3 Probabilities: continuous spectrum

If eigenstates \(|q\rangle\) satisfy Dirac orthonormality, \[ c(q)=\langle q|\psi\rangle, \] then probability of landing in \([q,q+dq]\) is: \[ P(q\in[q,q+dq]) = |c(q)|^2\,dq. \]

7.4 5.4 Collapse (state update)

After measuring \(Q\) and obtaining a value \(q\), the state “collapses” to the corresponding eigenstate (discrete: \(|q_n\rangle\); continuous: \(|q\rangle\), in an idealized sense).

7.5 5.5 Recovering position and momentum rules

  • Position basis: \(\psi(x)=\langle x|\psi\rangle\), and \(P(x\in[x,x+dx])=|\psi(x)|^2dx\).
  • Momentum basis: \(\phi(p)=\langle p|\psi\rangle\), and \(P(p\in[p,p+dp])=|\phi(p)|^2dp\).

The map \(\psi(x)\leftrightarrow \phi(p)\) is a Fourier transform: \[ \phi(p) \propto \int e^{-ipx/\hbar}\,\psi(x)\,dx, \qquad \psi(x) \propto \int e^{ipx/\hbar}\,\phi(p)\,dp. \]

8 6. The uncertainty principle

8.1 6.1 Uncertainty as standard deviation

For any observable \(A\), \[ (\Delta A)^2 = \langle (\hat A-\langle A\rangle)^2\rangle. \] Important: \(\Delta A\) does not mean your measurement device is imprecise; it means repeated measurements on identically prepared systems have an intrinsic spread.

8.2 6.2 Generalized uncertainty relation

Define \(\hat A'=\hat A-\langle A\rangle\), \(\hat B'=\hat B-\langle B\rangle\). Using Schwarz inequality with \(f=\hat A'\psi\), \(g=\hat B'\psi\), one obtains: \[ \Delta A\,\Delta B \;\ge\; \frac{1}{2}\,|\langle [\hat A,\hat B]\rangle|. \] This is the generalized uncertainty principle.

8.3 6.3 Heisenberg uncertainty as a special case

For \(\hat x\) and \(\hat p\), \[ [\hat x,\hat p]=i\hbar \quad\Rightarrow\quad \Delta x\,\Delta p \ge \frac{\hbar}{2}. \]

8.4 6.4 Compatible vs incompatible observables

  • If \([\hat A,\hat B]=0\), observables are compatible and can have a complete set of simultaneous eigenstates.
  • If \([\hat A,\hat B]\neq 0\), they are incompatible and cannot (in general) be simultaneously determinate.

Operational viewpoint: a measurement that collapses the state into an eigenstate of \(A\) typically destroys any prior definiteness of \(B\), unless the observables are compatible.

9 7. Minimum-uncertainty wave packets

9.1 7.1 When does equality hold?

To saturate the bound, the relevant vectors must be proportional: \[ \hat A'\psi = c\,\hat B'\psi \] with the constant constrained so the “dropped” piece vanishes.

9.2 7.2 For \(x\) and \(p\): the Gaussian result

For the position-momentum case, the condition becomes a differential equation whose general solution is a Gaussian: \[ \psi(x)\propto \exp\!\big(-a(x-x_0)^2 + i k_0 x\big), \] and these satisfy: \[ \Delta x\,\Delta p = \frac{\hbar}{2}. \] So Gaussian wave packets are the most general minimum-uncertainty states (at a given instant).

10 8. Energy–time uncertainty (and why it’s different)

10.1 8.1 Time is not an operator (nonrelativistic QM)

Unlike \(x,p,E\), time \(t\) is the independent parameter in the Schrödinger equation, not an observable represented by an operator in the same way. So \(\Delta t\) is not a standard deviation of “time measurements.”

10.2 8.2 Generalized Ehrenfest theorem (commutator dynamics)

For an observable \(\hat Q\), \[ \frac{d}{dt}\langle Q\rangle = \frac{i}{\hbar}\langle [\hat H,\hat Q]\rangle \quad \text{(if $\hat Q$ has no explicit $t$-dependence).} \]

10.3 8.3 Mandelstam–Tamm form of energy–time uncertainty

Take the generalized uncertainty relation with \(A=H\) and \(B=Q\). Using the previous result, one obtains: \[ \Delta E\,\Delta t \gtrsim \frac{\hbar}{2}, \] where \(\Delta t\) is interpreted as a timescale for significant change in \(\langle Q\rangle\), e.g. \[ \Delta t \sim \frac{\Delta Q}{\left|d\langle Q\rangle/dt\right|}. \] Key meaning: rapid evolution requires large energy spread; stationary states (\(\Delta E=0\)) have time-independent expectation values.

11 9. Vectors and operators: bases, matrices, and Dirac notation

11.1 9.1 States are abstract vectors (basis-independent)

The wavefunction \(\psi(x)\) is not the state itself; it’s the representation of the state in the position basis: \[ \psi(x) = \langle x|\psi\rangle. \] Similarly, momentum-space wavefunction: \[ \phi(p)=\langle p|\psi\rangle. \] And for a discrete energy basis \(\{|n\rangle\}\): \[ c_n = \langle n|\psi\rangle. \] All contain the same information: they are just components in different bases.

11.2 9.2 Operators as linear transformations

Operators map vectors to vectors: \[ \hat A: |\psi\rangle \mapsto |\psi'\rangle. \] Once a basis \(\{|n\rangle\}\) is chosen, the operator is represented by matrix elements: \[ A_{mn}=\langle m|\hat A|n\rangle. \] Then components transform exactly like matrix multiplication.

11.3 9.3 Finite-dimensional systems (e.g., two-level system)

If the system admits only \(N\) linearly independent states, everything reduces to standard linear algebra:

  • state = \(N\)-component column vector,
  • observable = \(N\times N\) Hermitian matrix,
  • time evolution = solve Schrödinger equation with that matrix Hamiltonian.

11.4 9.4 Dirac notation (bras, kets, outer products)

Dirac splits the inner product “bracket”:

  • Ket \(|\psi\rangle\): a vector
  • Bra \(\langle \psi|\): a linear functional acting on kets to give a complex number: \[ \langle \phi|\psi\rangle. \]

Outer product (rank-1 operator): \[ |\phi\rangle\langle \psi| \] acts as: it “projects onto \(|\psi\rangle\)” and outputs along \(|\phi\rangle\) (up to normalization).

Projection operator onto a normalized \(|\alpha\rangle\): \[ \hat P_\alpha = |\alpha\rangle\langle \alpha|. \]

11.5 9.5 Resolution of the identity (completeness)

For a discrete orthonormal basis: \[ \sum_n |n\rangle\langle n| = \hat I. \] For a continuous basis: \[ \int |x\rangle\langle x|\,dx = \hat I, \qquad \int |p\rangle\langle p|\,dp = \hat I. \]

11.6 9.6 Changing bases cleanly

Insert \(\hat I\) to transform components between bases: \[ \phi(p)=\langle p|\psi\rangle =\int \langle p|x\rangle\,\langle x|\psi\rangle\,dx =\int \langle p|x\rangle\,\psi(x)\,dx. \] Recognizing \(\langle x|p\rangle\) as the momentum eigenfunction in \(x\)-space yields the Fourier transform.

Operators transform similarly: \[ \langle p|\hat x|\psi\rangle = \int \langle p|x\rangle\,x\,\langle x|\psi\rangle\,dx, \] leading to the momentum-space form of the position operator.

12 10. One-page cheat sheet (key formulas)

12.1 States, probabilities, expectation values

  • \(\psi(x)=\langle x|\psi\rangle\)
  • \(P(x\in[x,x+dx])=|\psi(x)|^2dx\)
  • \(\langle A\rangle = \langle \psi|\hat A|\psi\rangle\)

12.2 Determinate states

  • \(\Delta A=0 \iff \hat A|\psi\rangle=a|\psi\rangle\)

12.3 Discrete spectral expansion

  • \(|\psi\rangle=\sum_n c_n|a_n\rangle\), \(c_n=\langle a_n|\psi\rangle\)
  • \(P(a_n)=|c_n|^2\)

12.4 Continuous spectral expansion

  • \(c(a)=\langle a|\psi\rangle\)
  • \(P(a\in[a,a+da])=|c(a)|^2 da\)

12.5 Uncertainty principle

  • \(\Delta A\,\Delta B \ge \frac12 |\langle[\hat A,\hat B]\rangle|\)
  • \(\Delta x\,\Delta p \ge \hbar/2\)

12.6 Minimum-uncertainty packets

  • Equality for \(x,p\) \(\Rightarrow\) Gaussian \(\psi(x)\)

12.7 Dynamics (generalized Ehrenfest)

  • \(\dfrac{d}{dt}\langle Q\rangle = \dfrac{i}{\hbar}\langle [\hat H,\hat Q]\rangle\) (if \(\partial \hat Q/\partial t=0\))

12.8 Completeness (identity)

  • \(\sum_n |n\rangle\langle n|=\hat I\), \(\int |x\rangle\langle x|dx=\hat I\)
